Siberian Tundra

Overview

The siberian tundra is located exclusively in Russia, as shown in this map. It is on the north of the country, stretching across most of the width of Asia. The absolute location is approximately 60°N to 80°N latitude and 90°E to 180°E longitude.

This website is intended to be viewed on widescreen devices

Climate & Characteristics

The Siberian tundra experiences extremely cold temperatures, long winters, and short, cool summers. Vegetation is sparse, and the landscape is dominated by permafrost. The season I chose is winter, as it is a defining season of the tundra biome. Winter is the longest and harshest season, typically lasting from October to April. During winter, temperatures can drop to as low as -40°C and -60°C. The tundra region is considered a cold desert, reaching less than 250 mm of annual rainfall, mostly in forms of snow.

In winter, the Siberian tundra desert experiences polar nights, where the sun remains below the horizon for weeks or even months, depending on the latitude. In December, some areas may get only a few hours of twilight per day, while others experience complete darkness.

Lichens are hardy, slow-growing organisms that survive extreme cold and low nutrient availability. Dwarf Birch are small shrubs, which are adapted to withstand freezing temperatures and permafrost conditions.

The Arctic Fox is a well-adapted predator with thick fur that changes color from brown in summer to white in winter for camouflage. Reindeers are herbivores that migrate and feed on lichens and mosses.
